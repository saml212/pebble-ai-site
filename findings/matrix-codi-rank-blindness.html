research note  ·  finding 05  ·  negative result

Matrix-CODI is decorative: four flat curves and a linear-algebra reason.

Sam Larson

pebble, San Francisco

April 14, 2026  ·  sam@pebbleml.com

abstract

Rizvi-Martel et al., The Illusion of Superposition? [8], recently showed empirically that a fine-tuned COCONUT-style latent reasoner reaches 96.6% on ProsQA without latent tokens, matching the full latent model to within a point and beating explicit chain-of-thought (85.3%) by a wide margin. The empirical claim that the latent reasoning machinery is not doing work in fine-tuned settings is theirs, and we do not reproduce it — our vanilla supervised fine-tune on ProsQA reaches only 81.77% (3-seed mean), roughly 15 percentage points below their published baseline. Against that baseline gap, we report a structural result that complements the Illusion finding. We built a fork of CODI with a d × d matrix bottleneck at each latent step, trained it on GSM8K-Aug and ProsQA at GPT-2 small scale, and measured the effective rank of the matrix thought Z. Across four orthogonal training conditions — two tasks, two distillation regimes, two thinker settings — the rank-k projection ablation curve is flat to within 0.6 percentage points. There is a linear-algebra reason: the forward pass reads Z as w_down(Z.flatten()), a linear map whose Jacobian with respect to Z is a constant tensor independent of Z. No gradient signal can reward Z's singular structure, so the model places its useful information in a single component and every downstream rank is decoration. Nine architectural variants we designed to break this — K-probe bilinear readouts, quadratic ZZ⊤ heads, spectral polynomial features, matrix-valued attention, an end-to-end matrix LM head, train-time rank constraints — all reduce to linear-in-Z under reshape and inherit the same rank-blindness. The contribution is the structural argument: any flatten-then-project readout on a matrix latent is rank-blind by construction, giving a specific mechanism for the empirical observation in [8] and closing off a family of "matrix bottleneck" architectural fixes without running them. Our absolute numbers sit ~15 points (ProsQA) to ~36 points (GSM8K-Aug) below the published state of the art, and we do not claim to have reproduced any published baseline; we make no claim that depends on absolute accuracy.

01What was already known, and what this note adds

Two 2025 theory papers argued that continuous chain-of-thought models hold multiple reasoning paths in superposition. Reasoning by Superposition [3] proved that a two-layer transformer with D steps of continuous thought can solve directed graph reachability, where each continuous thought encodes a parallel BFS frontier. CoT2 [4] showed similar parallel-exploration behavior under a different training regime. Both claims are theoretical and supported by accuracy curves. Neither work measures the structural property its construction describes, because vector representations don't have a single-point-in-time analog to rank — "how many patterns are active" in a vector has to be estimated across an ensemble.

A 2026 paper, The Illusion of Superposition? by Rizvi-Martel et al. [8], pushed back on the empirical side. Their headline numbers on ProsQA are the relevant prior art for anything we say below:

We did not reproduce the Illusion result. Our own vanilla GPT-2 supervised fine-tune on ProsQA reaches 81.77% (three-seed mean), roughly 15 percentage points below their 96.6%. The likely reasons are mundane — different hyperparameters, shorter training, different prompt format, different base model scale — and we explicitly disclaim any attempt to match their absolute number (see §11). The empirical "latents are decorative on ProsQA" finding is theirs. Nothing in this note should be read as an independent confirmation of the empirical claim, and nothing below depends on matching their absolute accuracy.

What we do contribute is a structural mechanism. Matrix-valued thoughts have a structural observable that vector thoughts don't: a d × d matrix has a rank, computable from a single sample via the SVD. The natural extension of the superposition hypothesis to matrix thoughts is that rank tracks the number of reasoning paths the model holds, and that projecting Z to lower rank at evaluation should hurt accuracy on tasks that need more paths. We ran that experiment in four training configurations and got flat curves in all of them. Then we worked out why — at the level of linear algebra — and the answer turned out to close off a family of architectural fixes without running them. That structural argument is what this note is about.

Framed carefully: The Illusion of Superposition? [8] shows empirically that fine-tuned latent reasoners don't need the latent machinery. This note shows structurally, within a matrix-valued variant of that family, why the machinery cannot carry the particular quantity (rank) that the superposition hypothesis predicts it should carry. The two findings are complementary, not competing, and the empirical observation is theirs.

02The architecture we tested

We fork CODI [5] and insert a matrix bottleneck into each latent reasoning step. CODI is the 2025 self-distillation framework that compresses explicit chain-of-thought into 6 continuous latent positions, matching explicit CoT on GSM8K at GPT-2 scale. The student pass feeds the previous latent position's hidden state back as the next input embedding, six times, before producing the answer.

The matrix bottleneck sits on that feedback path. Given a D-dimensional hidden state h, it projects to a d × d matrix, optionally applies one iteration of a multiplicative thinking layer, then projects back to D:

h_in  ∈ R^D                            # previous latent hidden state
flat  = w_up(h_in)                     # R^D → R^{d²}, Linear(D, d²)
Z     = flat.view(d, d)                # reshape to d × d
Z     = (I + Δ(Z)) · Z · (I + Γ(Z))    # optional thinker
flat' = Z.reshape(d²)                  # flatten back
h_out = LayerNorm(w_down(flat'))       # R^{d²} → R^D, Linear(d², D) + LN
return h_out, Z                        # h_out feeds the next step;
                                       # Z is saved for rank analysis

We use d = 16 throughout, giving a 256-dimensional flattened matrix and a 768-dimensional GPT-2 hidden. The multiplicative thinker (I + Δ)·Z·(I + Γ) is the same row/column update from our earlier matrix-thinking experiments. The forward implementation lives in matrix-thinking/scripts/run_matrix_codi.py, class MatrixBottleneck.

At evaluation we can optionally truncate Z to rank k via SVD before the down-projection: Zk = U:k Σ:k V:k⊤. This is the rank-k ablation. If rank is functional, accuracy at k = 1 should be meaningfully lower than accuracy at k = d = 16. If rank is vestigial, the two are the same.

03Four flat curves

We ran the bottleneck under four training conditions that vary the task, the CODI distillation weight γ, and the multiplicative thinker. Each run produced a single rank-k ablation curve. All four are flat.

The important property of this table is that it varies four things and none of them moves the curve:

  • Task. Arithmetic (GSM8K-Aug) and logical reasoning (ProsQA) differ by orders of magnitude in accuracy and by a factor of two in the mean effective rank the bottleneck learns. Both curves are flat.
  • CODI distillation. Setting γ = 0 removes the L1-at-colon loss that aligns the student's hidden state to the teacher's. This was the most-suspected training-side explanation for the flat curve. Round 3 Run 1 removes it and the curve is still flat; the effective rank rises (~10 → ~13) because the training signal is softer, but the functional rank does not.
  • Multiplicative thinker. Turning the thinker off (Round 3 Run 2) drops accuracy by roughly one point. It does not change rank dynamics and does not change curve shape.
  • Effective rank magnitude. The ProsQA bottleneck reaches effective rank ~12.8 out of a 16-dimensional ceiling. A rank-k projection to k = 1 destroys essentially all of that structure. Accuracy moves by 0.2 points. Whatever the model is using the matrix for, it is not using twelve independent singular directions.
result 1 The matrix bottleneck learns task-dependent effective rank (5.5 on arithmetic, 12.8 on logic), but that rank is not functionally read. Projecting Z to its top singular component at evaluation changes accuracy by less than 0.6 percentage points on every run we tested. Rank is decoration.

04Our local vanilla-SFT control (not a reproduction)

The Illusion of Superposition [8] result already tells us that, at a properly tuned operating point, the latent machinery is not doing work on ProsQA. We nevertheless ran the same-style control inside our own training pipeline so that Result 1 above can be compared against a same-distribution baseline, not an external number from a different codebase, model, and hyperparameter setup. The point of this section is not to rediscover the Illusion finding. It is to check that, within our weaker operating point, removing the entire matrix-CODI stack also does not hurt — so that the flat rank-k curves in §3 cannot be written off as "the stack is doing something even if rank isn't the mechanism."

We ran a three-seed control: vanilla GPT-2 small, no latents, no special tokens, no CODI distillation, no matrix bottleneck. Format is "question + ' The answer is:' → answer", standard cross-entropy on the full sequence, same hyperparameters as the matrix-CODI runs (lr = 1e-4, 25 epochs, batch 16, AdamW, identical data pipeline).

Two of three vanilla seeds tie the best matrix-CODI run. The mean across seeds is 0.26 percentage points below it. The matrix-CODI stack has 124.84M parameters; vanilla GPT-2 has 124.44M. Within our pipeline, the 0.4M additional parameters and the entire student–teacher distillation pipeline buy less than one-third of a percentage point on this task.

Our 81.77% vanilla SFT number is about 15 percentage points below the 96.6% figure Rizvi-Martel et al. [8] report for fine-tuned COCONUT without latent tokens on ProsQA. Possible reasons: different hyperparameters, shorter training, different prompt formatting, different data preprocessing. We have not closed the gap. The Illusion of Superposition paper is the stronger empirical baseline for the "latents are decorative" claim, and that empirical claim is theirs. Our local vanilla-SFT number shows the narrower statement in Result 2: inside our training pipeline, at our weaker operating point, the matrix-CODI stack is also not contributing. That is what Result 1's flat curves need it to show, and no more.

result 2 (local, same-distribution only) Inside our own training pipeline, a vanilla GPT-2 supervised fine-tune on ProsQA matches matrix-CODI within 0.26 percentage points averaged over three seeds, and ties the matrix-CODI best on two of three seeds. Both sit ~15 points below the published state of the art [8]. Reading: within our setup, the matrix-CODI stack contributes nothing above vanilla fine-tuning; we make no cross-setup claim. The empirical "fine-tuned latent reasoners don't use their latents" finding is due to [8], not this note.

Two readings of the local control are compatible with our evidence. First, the narrow one: ProsQA at GPT-2 small and our operating point is solvable by shortcut pattern-matching on the question tokens, so neither the latents nor the matrix have anything to contribute. Second, the broader one: the superposition hypothesis predicts that continuous thoughts carry structural information about parallel reasoning paths, and a model that is not using that structural information will match a model that has no thoughts at all. The Illusion paper's properly-tuned 96.6% / 99.0% numbers [8] are the evidence for the broader reading at the right operating point; our local control only rules out "something else in the matrix-CODI stack is compensating" within our setup. Result 1, the four flat rank-k curves, tells us what mechanism is relevant inside our specific architecture: the matrix does build rank, but nothing in the forward pass reads it.

05Result 3: what is Z encoding

The flat rank-k curves in §3 and the vanilla-SFT tie in §4 leave open a question: if the matrix bottleneck is not using rank and is not contributing accuracy, what does Z carry? We ran a linear probe to check.

Setup. Load the Round 3 Run 1 checkpoint (matrix-CODI, γ = 0, thinker on, ProsQA). Run a forward pass on 500 held-out ProsQA test problems and extract the matrix thought Z at all 6 latent positions, giving 3000 Z tensors. Flatten each to ℝ256. Train a 5-fold cross-validated multi-class logistic regression that predicts the target class symbol (the correct answer identity in the ProsQA ontology) from Z, reporting macro-averaged one-vs-rest AUC. Two controls run on the same 500 problems and the same prompt: vanilla pretrained GPT-2 (no ProsQA fine-tuning) hidden state at the final prompt token, and a randomly initialized GPT-2 hidden state at the same position.

The pre-registered threshold was: matrix Z AUC must exceed max(vanilla, random) + 0.05 to count as a positive result.

Verdict: null. The best matrix Z AUC (0.693) does not exceed the threshold (0.896). Three facts the table shows:

  1. Vanilla GPT-2, untrained on ProsQA, predicts the target class symbol with AUC 0.846. The trained matrix-CODI bottleneck reaches 0.673. The bottleneck is a strict compression of the hidden state through w_up: Linear(768, 256) → reshape → w_down: Linear(256, 768), and that compression loses target-predictive information relative to the uncompressed hidden state. After fine-tuning on ProsQA, the compressed representation carries less target information than the uncompressed pretrained representation of the same input.
  2. Per-position AUC peaks at Z[1] (0.693) and decays to Z[5] (0.633). Later latent positions encode less target-predictive information than earlier ones. A story in which the matrix thought deepens over successive latent steps would predict the opposite shape.
  3. A binary target-vs-neg_target probe on the same Z tensors — asking whether the candidate that Z points to is the correct answer or the distractor in the diamond DAG — produces AUCs between 0.50 and 0.56 across all conditions. Chance. Matrix Z cannot distinguish the correct answer candidate from its negation.

The probe result is consistent with the rank-blindness derivation in §6. If the loss gradient cannot reward Z's singular structure, the matrix ends up encoding whatever happens to satisfy w_down's linear readout, which in this checkpoint is neither the target class nor the correct candidate identity. The bottleneck does not construct reasoning state; it compresses the question through a rank-agnostic linear map, and the compression loses information that the uncompressed pretrained hidden state already carries about the answer.

result 3 A linear probe on the Round 3 Run 1 matrix thought Z predicts the ProsQA target class with AUC 0.673, below the pre-registered threshold of 0.896 set by the vanilla pretrained GPT-2 hidden state (0.846). A binary probe distinguishing correct answer from distractor is at chance (0.50–0.56) across all conditions. The matrix bottleneck does not encode the answer.

06Why the readout is rank-blind

The forward pass reads Z through exactly one operation: h_out = w_down(Z.flatten()), where w_down is a Linear(d², D) layer followed by a LayerNorm. Write w = vec(W) for the flattened weights and z = vec(Z) for the flattened thought. The readout is

h_out = LayerNorm(W · z + b)
      = LayerNorm(Σ_ij W[:, ij] · Z[i, j] + b)

Drop the LayerNorm for a moment (it is a post-hoc normalization that does not depend on Z's singular structure). The map Z ↦ W · vec(Z) is linear in Z. Its Jacobian

∂h_out / ∂Z[i, j] = W[:, ij]        (constant tensor)

does not depend on Z. The loss gradient with respect to Z at a given step is therefore

∂L / ∂Z = (∂L / ∂h_out) · (∂h_out / ∂Z)
        = some vector, contracted with a constant tensor

which cannot depend on the singular structure of Z itself. From the optimizer's point of view, there is no gradient pressure that says "Z should have two independent singular directions rather than one." If the model can place its useful information in a single direction and still minimize the loss, it will, because that is the path of least resistance during training.

That is exactly what we observe. Across every condition in Table 1, the mean effective rank rises during training (because the w_up projection is full-rank and noise alone produces non-degenerate matrices), but the functionally used rank is 1. Truncating to the top singular component preserves the accuracy because the top singular component is where the model put the answer.

The same argument closes off a larger family of readouts. Any function f(Z) that is linear in Z — or that factors through a linear map on a fixed-size space derived from Z, like a flatten, a reshape, or the symmetric Gram ZZ⊤ — has a Jacobian with respect to Z that is constant in Z. The loss gradient through any such readout cannot reward Z's singular structure. We call this rank-blindness by linear algebra.

structural finding Any readout that is linear in Z has a Jacobian that is constant in Z, so the loss gradient carries no rank information. Bilinear K-probes, Frobenius-inner-product readouts, low-rank factored w_down, and per-vocab matrix LM heads all fall inside this family. Every one of them is rank-blind.

07Nine proposals, all killed by the same argument

After Round 3 finished flat we ran an attack-agent waterfall on every candidate fix we could think of. Nine distinct architectural variants went in. None of them survived to a GPU launch. Each one failed for either the rank-blindness argument above, a different kind of linear-algebra mistake, or a capacity confound that made the resulting experiment unfalsifiable.

The waterfall took about a day. Nothing in the table reached the GPU, and in retrospect that was the correct decision: eight of the nine would have produced another flat curve for the same reason Rounds 1–3 did, and the ninth (MNNS) was built on a paper that turned out to have a fabricated github link. The experiment budget was better spent running the vanilla SFT control in Round 4 than on any of these.

08Four lessons

Together the nine killed proposals and the four flat curves produce a compressed generalization of what broke. We record it in the project's KILL_LIST as four lessons; here is the short form.

  1. Lesson 1 — linear-in-Z is rank-blind. Any operation whose Jacobian with respect to Z is constant in Z cannot produce rank-aware gradients. Flatten, reshape, Frobenius inner product, low-rank factorization of the readout weights, and symmetric Z Z⊤ all fall in this family.
  2. Lesson 2 — the model always finds the lowest-rank representation that satisfies the loss. Adding a more rank-aware readout does not force rank usage. It makes rank usage possible, and the model still picks the easiest path, which is rank-1.
  3. Lesson 3 — train-time rank constraints don't help. Forcing Z to be rank-r by parameterization does not change the gradient signal, so it does not change what the model chooses to put in those r components. A rank-r constrained Z with a rank-blind readout is equivalent to a rank-1 unconstrained one.
  4. Lesson 4 — rank discrimination requires one of four things. An explicit loss term that rewards or penalizes rank (unfalsifiable without care), a task whose ground truth provably requires K independent quantities and has no rank-1 shortcut (we have not yet found one at this scale), a different question entirely (probing what is in Z via interpretability), or a fundamentally different mechanism where matrix structure participates in the forward pass in a way that no linear map can flatten.

09Relation to prior work

This note sits downstream of three lines of work and complements — rather than replicates — the empirical thread that matters most.

The Illusion of Superposition? (Rizvi-Martel et al. [8], 2026). This is the load-bearing prior art. Their paper's headline empirical finding on ProsQA — 99.0% with latents, 96.6% without latents, 85.3% for explicit chain-of-thought — already establishes that the latent reasoning machinery contributes ~2.4 points on top of shortcut-fine-tuning and that the shortcut-fine-tuned model blows past explicit CoT by over 11 points. Their evidence comes from running the fine-tuned model with and without latent tokens, and from logit-lens and entity-level probing. We do not claim the "latents are decorative" empirical result; it is theirs. Our local same-distribution vanilla-SFT control in §4 is not a reproduction of their number — we sit ~15 points below their 96.6%, at a weaker operating point — and should not be cited as independent confirmation of their empirical finding.

What this note adds on top of [8] is a structural mechanism: a Jacobian-level argument that explains why, within the matrix-valued bottleneck family, no architecture whose readout reduces to a linear map on vec(Z) can use Z's rank as a functional quantity, regardless of the task, the loss, or the readout's weight tying. The Illusion paper asks "are the latents doing work?" and answers empirically "no." We ask a narrower follow-up: "could a matrix-valued latent do work through its singular structure?" and answer structurally "no, not under any readout in the linear-in-Z family, by linear algebra." The two findings are compatible and independent: one is a behavioral test, the other is a calculation on the architecture.

Reasoning by Superposition [3] and CoT2 [4]. These papers motivate the rank-tracks-reasoning hypothesis from the theoretical side. [3] proves a capacity result (two layers + D continuous-thought steps solve graph reachability, where D is graph diameter) and provides small-scale empirical evidence that the learned solution uses superposition when a model is trained end-to-end on the task. Our setup is different along multiple axes: pretrained backbone, ProsQA / GSM8K-Aug rather than graph reachability, CODI-style L1-at-colon distillation, and — critically — the rank-blindness we identify comes from the matrix bottleneck's readout, not from the underlying transformer. A standard vector transformer on a standard training regime has no matrix to read or misread. Our negative result is specifically about whether matrix rank can measure superposition under a flatten-then-project architecture, not a disproof that superposition ever occurs. Our experiments always start from pretrained GPT-2; the from-scratch regime of [8] (which is the only regime where they found positive evidence for superposition) and the end-to-end regime of [3] are distinct questions and we have not run them.

What's new here, precisely. The structural argument in §6 is, as far as we can find, not in the literature. Prior negative results in latent CoT have reported that accuracy fails to track the theoretical construction, or that probing reveals shortcut behavior. We give a reason at the linear-algebra level: any readout whose Jacobian with respect to the matrix thought is constant in the thought cannot produce a gradient that rewards the thought's singular structure. Rank-blindness is a property of the architecture family, not a property of the training procedure, the task, or the loss weighting. This is the piece that the empirical thread ([8]) does not supply and that this note adds.

10What this result is, precisely

Stated narrowly so the claim is defensible:

  • Empirical (local, our pipeline only). At GPT-2 small scale (124M backbone), on GSM8K-Aug and ProsQA, with d = 16 matrix thoughts, across four combinations of distillation weight and multiplicative thinker, the rank-k projection ablation curve is flat within 0.6 percentage points and the Spearman rank correlation between per-sample effective rank and correctness is within ±0.11 of zero with inconsistent sign. Inside the same pipeline, a vanilla GPT-2 supervised fine-tune with no latents, no distillation, and no matrix matches the best matrix-CODI run within 0.26 percentage points averaged over three seeds. Both matrix-CODI and our vanilla SFT sit ~15 points below the published state of the art on ProsQA [8] and ~36 points below on GSM8K [5].
  • Structural (the contribution). The forward pass reads Z through a linear-in-Z map, whose Jacobian is constant in Z, so the loss gradient cannot reward rank. Any readout whose Jacobian with respect to Z is constant in Z — including all eight of the non-MNNS variants in Table 4 — inherits the same property. The structural argument does not depend on our operating point, our training procedure, our tasks, or our loss weighting. It is a statement about the architecture family.

What the result does not say:

  • It does not say that fine-tuned latent reasoners are empirically decorative on ProsQA. That claim is due to Rizvi-Martel et al. [8] at a stronger operating point. Our local control is consistent with it, but does not establish it; the baseline gap in §11 is too large for us to make that claim independently.
  • It does not say that continuous chain-of-thought models in general fail to use superposition. The from-scratch regime of [8] is evidence that they can, in the right setting.
  • It does not say that matrix-valued representations are useless. Our earlier outer-product embedding result [see outer-product embedding] shows that matrix representations can beat flat vectors per parameter by 11× at single-step processing. The negative result here is specifically about rank as a functional observable in a flatten-then-project readout, not about matrix structure overall.
  • It does not say that ProsQA is unsolvable by structured reasoning. It says the structured reasoning machinery in our specific stack, under a linear-in-Z readout, cannot use Z's rank to do the solving.
  • It does not generalize beyond GPT-2 small. The rank-blindness argument holds at any scale, but the question of whether the claim would matter — whether rank is the quantity that carries parallel reasoning state — is open at larger scale and under architectures outside the linear-in-Z readout family.

11Caveats and limitations

This note is a research note, not a peer-reviewed paper. The absolute numbers:

  • Our ProsQA baseline is ~15 points below the published state of the art. Rizvi-Martel et al. [8] report 96.6% for fine-tuned COCONUT without latent tokens on ProsQA. Our vanilla GPT-2 supervised fine-tune reaches 81.77% (three-seed mean). Our best matrix-CODI run reaches 82.03%. We have not closed this gap. Possible reasons include different hyperparameters, shorter training, different prompt formatting, different evaluation protocol, and possibly different data preprocessing; we have not done the systematic ablation that would identify which. We do not claim to have reproduced the Illusion of Superposition baseline, and nothing in the structural argument of §6 depends on matching their number. If a reviewer reads Result 2 as "the latents are decorative on ProsQA," they should read that claim as already made by [8] at a much stronger operating point, not as something this note establishes independently.
  • Our GSM8K-Aug number is ~36 points below published CODI. CODI [5] reports 43.7% on GSM8K; our Round 1 Run A (vanilla CODI setup, same codebase as the matrix runs) reached 7.00%. The gap is large enough that GSM8K-Aug in our setup should be read as a low-accuracy regime in which the model is barely learning the task at all, not as a reproduction of any published result. The only claim we make on GSM8K-Aug is Table 1's flat rank-k curve at that low-accuracy operating point.
  • The negative result is robust to seed variance within our setup, but our setup is at a weaker operating point than the literature. The flat rank-k curves in Table 1 and the matching of matrix-CODI and vanilla SFT in Table 2 are reliable statements about our specific training pipeline. A reader should not conclude from them alone that the stronger-operating-point results of [8] would look the same under our measurements, because we do not have data in that regime. What we do claim is that the structural argument in §6 would apply at any operating point under any linear-in-Z readout, because it is a statement about Jacobians, not about training curves.
  • Single backbone. Everything runs on GPT-2 small 124M. The literature's strongest positive evidence for superposition in continuous thought comes from models of comparable scale trained from scratch on the reasoning task. A larger backbone would change the accuracy floor but not the structural argument.
  • Two tasks. GSM8K-Aug and ProsQA were chosen because one is arithmetic and one is logical reasoning. Both are tasks where the theoretical superposition story should matter. A synthetic task whose ground truth provably requires K linearly independent quantities would be a stronger test of the hypothesis itself. We have not yet built such a task at the right scale (see Lesson 4 and the MNNS failure in Table 4).
  • Effective rank, not exact rank. We use the exponential of singular-value entropy as a smooth proxy for rank. All our conclusions would be the same under a hard singular-value threshold, but effective rank is the differentiable version that training-time probes require.
  • Sample efficiency (Round 5) and scale grid (Round 6) are in flight. Round 5 tests whether matrix-CODI is more sample-efficient than vanilla SFT in the low-data regime (where the matrix bottleneck's inductive bias might contribute even if it does not help at saturation). Round 6 sweeps d ∈ {16, 32, 64} to rule the matrix dimension in or out as a capacity confound. Neither experiment changes the Jacobian argument in §6 — which does not depend on our operating point — but both give the architecture additional chances to show a benefit we missed. Results will be added to this note when both complete.

12What we would try next

Given the rank-blindness argument, the surviving paths fall cleanly into four categories:

  1. A different question. Replace "does rank track reasoning?" with "what is encoded in Z?" via linear probing, entity-level probing, or causal interventions. This is the move the Illusion of Superposition paper makes, and our negative result suggests it is the more productive question at this scale.
  2. An explicit rank-aware objective. Train with a nuclear-norm or effective-rank term in the loss, at varying coefficient, and measure whether the induced rank changes accuracy on tasks whose solutions plausibly need multiple components. This is "unfalsifiable" in the cheap sense — of course rank goes up if you reward it — but it can be designed to test the stronger claim: that rewarding rank improves accuracy on the right kind of task, while hurting it on the wrong kind. That would be real evidence that rank is functional somewhere.
  3. A task whose ground truth provably requires multiple independent quantities. Synthetic reasoning problems where the solution has a verifiable K-frontier and no rank-1 shortcut. Designing these without the MNNS trap is open work.
  4. A different mechanism entirely. Matrix structure that participates in the forward pass in a way no linear map can flatten. The killed matrix-valued attention (#9) and matrix LM head (#8) were attempts at this that didn't survive the waterfall; a surviving version would need a non-linearity that acts on the matrix as a matrix rather than on its flattened vector form. We don't currently have a specific proposal here; it is marked as a longer-horizon direction.

None of these rescues the rank-as-superposition framing on its own. They give the hypothesis additional chances to produce a positive signal. If none of them do, the conclusion stands: the matrix-CODI program as originally framed was wrong about what rank measures, and the structural argument in §6 tells us why.
